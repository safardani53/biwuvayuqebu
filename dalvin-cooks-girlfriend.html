Who Is Tokyo Jetz?

Born on October 26, 1994, Tokyo Jetz is an American rapper for her hit single No Problem. She was born in Jacksonville, Florida.

Her birth name is Shauntrell Pender, but she later changes it to Tokyo Jetz. The reason behind unique names mainly for two reasons.

The name Tokyo comes from her Asian-like eyes, and Jetz is the name of one of her all-time favorite artists, Curren$y’s song.

She broke into the internet after uploading a series of freestyle rap videos in her car.

Jetz’s Parents, Siblings & Education

Tokyo Jetz’s parents divorced while she was young. Thus, she spent most of her childhood living with her dad, two older brothers, and a younger sister.

She once tried to move in with her mother, who lived in Atlanta during her high school years. But she was forced to live on her own, separately.

She did not receive much affection from her parents, but she still loves and treats them respectfully despite this fact.

Her father could not provide much as he took care of four children alone while her mother went away to another city, which deserves to be praised.

As an ordinary high school kid, she had no income source to live independently. So she ended up returning to Jacksonville and dropped out of high school so she could work and earn a couple of dollars to support her family.

She never graduated from high school; she only attended 11th grade. As a high schooler, she was interested in poetry and music and occasionally participated in inter-school competitions.

She may not have graduated from high school, but she sure is doing better in her life.

Early Career

She started as a poet in high school and gradually got into hip-hop and rap culture. It was one of her brothers who encouraged her to turn her poems into lyrical verses for rap.

In 2012, one of her songs became a hit in her hometown, which was probably her first break ever. Later, she decided to post content on social media platforms to grow her career as a rapper.

Luckily this decision proved to be a life-changer, literally. She gained popularity through her freestyle videos.

In 2016 she rapped over Yo Gotti’s Down in the DM with her original verse, which received quite a lot of attention and views.

Tokyo Jetz moved to Atlanta to further develop her musical career as a rapper in early 2017. Soon after, she went on a tour around the United States with fellow artists signed to Grand Hustle.

Jetz had an unfulfilling childhood; her parents were divorced, and her family did not have much to offer financially. Therefore, she grew up in a rough area of the city, as rents are usually cheaper in those areas.

Jetz stated that she remembers living off section 8 and food stamps. She did not have a happy childhood growing up in the projects.

Slowly but steadily, she grew her internet persona as a freestyle rapper who raps about the bitter truth of the street lifestyle. Her style seems aggressive, but she can also be high-spirited, depending on the mood and demand of the bars.

After being viral on the internet, she was noticed by T.I., a famous name in the rap industry. T.I. later signed her to Grand Hustle, a record label, in 2016.

Since then, she has collaborated with rappers such as T.I., Trina, and Kash Doll.

Tokyo did her event for 2022 at Enmarket Arena [Apr. 2nd, 2022], Black Station Presents Spring Fling 2022 by BEEZY ENT held in Savannah, GA.

Tokyo Jetz Faces The Wrath Of Critics

In the same year, she received quite a harsh backlash from her fans as she made an irresponsible and offensive comment while going live on her Instagram.

Tokyo made a joke about George Floyd’s death. She uploaded an Instagram story on June 1, not even a week after George’s death, making an offensive statement.

She was play-fighting with her friend and said, “I’m a George Floyd, your motherf***ing a**.”

After this, she received a lot of backlash plus a whole lot of criticism. She decided to record and upload an apology video, apologizing to the black community and all the people she hurt through the comment.

Jetz started the apology video by saying, “There is no excuse; that’s not what this is at all.” Followed by, “I’m not doing this video to ask for no sympathy cause what I did was wrong as hell.”

She added, “First and foremost, I want to just apologize to the family, to people on the frontlines, to people who stand up for us.”

Tokyo also stated that she does not want anyone to take her statements as an excuse.

This happened in July 2020 when cancel culture was on the rise. So many pointed out that she should be canceled. But luckily, that did not happen.

She responded to the criticism and wrath of the cancel culture she faced by releasing a Cancel Culture album in 2021.
